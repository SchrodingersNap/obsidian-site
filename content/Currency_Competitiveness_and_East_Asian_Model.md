# ðŸ’± Currency Competitiveness: The Structural Link

> [!ABSTRACT] The Central Thesis
> A strong currency is not a badge of honor to be bought with high interest rates; it is a **crown to be earned** through manufacturing competitiveness.
>
> The Survey argues that India must avoid the "Strong Currency Trap." Historical evidence from **East Asia** shows that durable currency credibility comes *after* a nation establishes itself as a manufacturing powerhouse, not before. Currently, India's reliance on **volatile capital flows** (to fund the CAD) makes the Rupee vulnerable, unlike the export-surplus economies of Japan or Korea.

---

## 1. The Great Debate: Does a Weak Rupee Help? (Box IV.5)

There is a perennial debate in Indian policy circles: *Should we let the Rupee slide to boost exports, or defend it to control inflation/debt costs?*

The Survey settles this with empirical evidence from an **ARDL Model** (Autoregressive Distributed Lag), analyzing data from FY12 to FY26.

| The Channel | The Mechanism | The Verdict (Elasticity) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Trade Channel** | Depreciation makes exports cheaper and imports expensive. | **$\epsilon = -1.45$**<br>(Highly Responsive. Weak Rupee significantly improves Trade Balance). |
| **Financial Channel** | Depreciation increases the repayment burden of foreign debt (ECBs). | **Net Negative impact**, but smaller than trade gains. |
| **Services Trade** | Impact of currency fluctuations on IT/Consulting. | **$\epsilon = -0.38$**<br>(Inelastic. Service exports depend on *skill/quality*, not just price). |

> [!TIP] The Policy Conclusion
> **"Trade gains outweigh financial costs."**
> For an economy like India, maintaining **export competitiveness** via a competitive exchange rate is more beneficial than artificially propping up the currency to lower debt service costs.

## 2. The East Asian Masterclass (Box IV.6)

How did Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan achieve "Hard Currency" status? It wasn't through financial wizardry. It was a disciplined, multi-decade cycle of **Industrial Policy**.

### Key Pillars of the East Asian Model
1. **Disciplined Support:** Protectionism was _temporary_ and linked to export performance. If a firm couldn't export, subsidies stopped.
2. **Upstream vs. Downstream:** They largely **did not protect** upstream industries (Steel/Petrochemicals) to ensure downstream exporters (Cars/Electronics) got cheap raw materials.
    - _Contrast with India:_ We often protect steel/polymers via Anti-Dumping Duties, hurting our SME engineering exporters.
3. **The Sequence:** They accepted a weak currency for decades to capture global market share. Appreciation only happened _after_ they became dominant.
---

## 3. India's Structural Flaw: The "Financial" Trap

Why is the Rupee volatile while the Yen or Won (historically) were stable?
- **East Asia:** Funded their economy through **Trade Surpluses** (Earned Income).
- **India:** Funds its growth through **Capital Inflows** (Borrowed/Invested Money).
    - We run a persistent **Merchandise Trade Deficit**.
    - We plug the gap with **Services Surplus** (Software) and **Capital Flows** (FDI/FPI).
    - _The Risk:_ Capital flows are fickle. When the US Fed raises rates, money leaves India, crashing the Rupee.

**The Net International Investment Position (NIIP):**
- India is a **Net Debtor** (Liabilities > Assets).
- Rank: 11th largest debtor globally.
- _Saving Grace:_ External debt is low (**18.4% of GDP**), and Forex reserves cover **94%** of it.
